\section{Introduction}

The comercial use of Unmanned Aerial Systems (UASs) in urban environments is expanding rapidly, with applications ranging from goods delivery and infrastucture inspection to survalance and sports coverage. \par

\vspace{1em}

Industry projections and regulatory road maps anticipate that the density of low altitude urban air traffic will within the next decade exceed what can be co-ordantied by human Air Traffic Contorllers (ATCs). This is why governments and regulatory bodies have developed frameworks for automated UAS Traffic Managment (UTM): in May 2025, the European Union Aviation Safety Agency certified its first U-space Service Provider \cite{easa2025anra}, and the United kingdom Civial Aviation Authoriy has published targets aimed at enabling urban UTM by 2028 \cite{caa2024bvlos}. Despite this the technical challenges of developing such an automated sytem and how it should perform remains an area of active research. \par

\vspace{1em}

A central part of the UTM problem is Airspace Service providers (ASPs) must be able to co-ordinate the flight plan requests within a defined sector of urban airspace \cite{kopardekar2014utm, faa2022utmconops}. Multiple ASPs are expected to operate concurrently in a networked system, managing the handover of of UASs between sectors by following well-defined protocols. The policies and protocols by which ASPs give autherisation decisions fundimentially shape the saftey and efficiency of the UTM system, yet the comparative behaviour of different policies under realistic demand patterns is not yet well understood. \par

\vspace{1em}

This project frames the problem of co-ordinating UASs through ASPs as a Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) problem.The problem of finding collision free paths for multiple agents sharing a descrete environment \cite{stern2019multi}. MAPF is a well studied problem with a large body of literature on algorithms for saftey, optimality and scalablity of different automated management policies. Aswell as a substantial number of potential candiadate algorithms ranging from simple heuristic searches to complex conflict resolving procedures. Treating the UTM problem as a MAPF problem makes it possable to evaluate polices systematially under quantified demand patterns to compare and contrast there performance (rather then judging them on intuition). \par

\vspace{1em}

To support this evaluation, a custom discrete-event simulaiton framework has been developed in python using SimPy. The simulaiton models a two-dimensional urban airspace divided into multiple grid sectors, each managed by an independed ASP and connected to its neighours by directional gates that abstract the handover protocols described in the UTM literature. UAVs operate as lifelong agents (as introduced by Ma et al \cite{ma2020lifelong}), picking up jobs from designated depots and travelling between depots and destinations that may be located within there orgin sector or in a different sector. Routing is structured heirarchically; a high level planner that resolves the airspaces the UAV must pass through, and a low level planner which is responsable for finding a path within a sector. The low level planner is suplimented with optional collision avoidance polices such as reservation based deconfliction and Recipricol Velocity Obstacles (RVO) \par

\vspace{1em}

The aim of this project is to evalutae the comparative performance of automated airpsace manegment polices for ASP controlled urban UAS traffic, with particular focus to the saftey and efficiency trade-off that distinguishes one policy from another. The associated objectives are to design and imliment a multi-ASP discrete-event simultion frameowrk, to define quantitative perfromance metrics coverving both saftey (seperation violations) and efficientcy (timeliness and throughput), to impliment a range of management policies, and to conduct a systematic evaluation of these polices under varying demand patterns and constraints. \par

\vspace{1em}

the remainder of this report is structured as followed: The remainder of this chapter reivews the literatire on single-agent planning, multi-agent planning, UAS traffic managment, and reservation baed deconfliction. Chapter 2 describes the design and methodology of the simulation framwork, includign the achritecture, the polices and the project managment approch adopted. Chapter 3 documents the implimentation, validationa nd testing of the simulaitn frameowrk. Chapter 4 presents the experimental results and discusses thier implicaitons for ASP polciy design. Finally Chapter 5 concludes the report and ideities directions for future work.\par

\section{Literature review}

This chapter reviews the relevant literature on which this project is based. The review straddles three research areas: algorithmic literature on pathfinding, operational and regulatory literature on UAS traffic management, and the enginnering literature on conflict resolution in multi-agent systems. The remainder of this chapter examines each and identies the specific gap this project aims to fill. \par

\subsection{Single-agent path planning}
\label{sec:single-agent-planning}

Single-agent path planning, the problem of computing a route between two points in a given environment subject to constraints (such as obstacles and cost functions), has been extensively studied as early as the 1950s and provides the foundation for the multi-agent path finding problem. This subsection introduces teh algorithms that appear as componets of the multi-agent path finding polcies evaluated in this project, and the literature on which they are based. \par

\vspace{1em}

Breadth-First Search (BFS) and Depth-First Search (DFS) are the most basic uninformed search algorithms for path planning \cite{cormen2022clrs}. BFS returns the path with the fewst steps from start to goal on a uniform cost grid, while DFS explores each branch to the maximum depth before backtracking. while both algorithms are complete neither is optimal, and both can be inefficient in large search spaces. they have been included in this project as a baseline for comparison with more sophiticated algorithms and to provide a measureable benchmark for which more complex algorithms can be compared against. \par

\vspace{1em}

The A* algorithm dominates the literature on single-agent path planning and is widely used in practice \cite{hart1968astar}. A* augments Dijkstras algorithm with a heuristic estimate of the remaining cost from each point to the goal, and is guaranteed to return an optimal path whenever this heuristic is admissible (never overestimates the true cost). By focusing the seach toward the goal rather than expanding all possible paths, therefor making A* substantially more efficient than uninformed algorithms like BFS and DFS. while retianing the optimality guaranteees. The implimentation in this project uses the euclidean distance (the straight-line distance) to the goal as its heuristic. \par

\vspace{1em}

While the A* algorithm is optimal for pathfinding, it doesnt account for the dynamic nature of the environment, such as moving obstacles or changing conditions. This is where the D* algorithm comes in \cite{stentz1994dstar} D* is an extension of A* that allows for replanning in dynamic environments. It maintains a cost map of the environment and updates it as new information becomes available, allowing it to efficiently replan paths when obstacles are detected or conditions change. Within the context of this project, D* lite is used which is an augmentation of D* that is more efficient and easier to implement \cite{koenig2004dstar} as it works backwards from the goal to the start making it more suitable for real-time applications (or simulations) as the end goal stays the same but the current state may change. \par

\vspace{1em}

The final single-agent path planning algorithm used in this project is a "greedy" algorithm. This algirthm always returns the straight line path between the start and goal, even if the path is not collision free. This algorithm is intended to provide a lowest baseline for comparision with other algorithms, as it is the most efficient in terms of computational time but has no guarantees of optimality or saftey. \par

\vspace{1em}

The single-agent path planning algorithms described in this section can plan one agents path through an evenronment that is static, however they do not account for the presence of other agents within the environment. When multiple agents share an environment, and their plans interact, applying a single agent path planning algoirhtm produces plans that may conflict at execution time. Resolving these conflicts is the problem of multi-agent path finding, which is the focus of this project and is discussed in the next section. \par

\subsection{Multi-agent path finding}
\label{sec:mapf}

Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) extends the single-agent path planning problem to environments in which multiple agents operate and their paths must not conflict with each other. Stern et al. \cite{stern2019mapf} define the MAPF problem as a set of agents, each with an assigned start and goal location, that must find paths to their respective goals without colliding with each other. The problem is typically modelled on a discrete grid or graph in which agents move to adjacent nodes at each time step. The standard objectives are makespan, defined as the time at which the last agent reaches its goal and flowtime, defined as the sum of individual agents path costs. The variant addressed in this work is non-anonymous, lifelong, and online: UAV identities are fixed, new flight plan requests arrive dynamically during execution, and each UAV operates a depot-bound task cycle in which it travels from its home depot to a pickup location, on to a delivery location, and back to its home depot before becoming available for a new job. This depot-bound structure is a constrained form of the lifelong Multi-Agent pickup and delivery formulation introduced for warehouse automation by Ma et al. \cite{ma2017lifelong}, in which agents receive new tasks immediately upon completing a delivery; the constraint reflects the operational realities of UAS delivery, where drones typically return to a depot between flights for recharging and maintenance. \par

\vspace{1em}

Prioritised planning, introduced by Erdmann and Lozano-P\'erez \cite{erdmann1987multiple}, assigns agents a priority order and plans their paths sequentially, each agent treating the planned paths of higher-priority agents as dynamic obstacles. This approach is simple and efficient, and can be very effective in practice, especially when the priority order is well-chosen. However despite this method being substantially cheaper then the methods i will discuss below, it is incomplete: a higher priority agents plan can block all remaining paths for a lower priority agent, even if a valid plan could be feasable given a different ordering. One way this is resolved, is by using Priority Based Search (PBS) \cite{ma2019pbs}, which achives completeness by searching systematically over partial priority orderings rather than commiting to a single fixed order. \par



\subsection{UAS Traffic Management}
\label{sec:utm}

The MAPF literature provides a range of algorithms in which multiple agents can be co-ordinated, but it does not address the specific context in which the project sits. The UAS Traffic Management (UTM) literature provides the framework for understanding the conventions, regulations and operational realities of UAS traffic in urban environments. \par

\vspace{1em}

The idea of UTM was first proposed by Kopardekar at NASA \cite{kopardekar2014utm} as a response to the anticipated growth of UAS operations in low-altitude operations. Kopardekar observed that current human controlled ATC infrastructure couldn't scale to the densities anticipated for commercial UAS operations. Therefor he proposed a new system of automated traffic management, in which multiple Airspace Service Providers (ASPs) would co-ordinate the flight plans of UASs within defined sectors of urban airspace. This proposal has since shaped the regulatory oversight and development of UTM systems. The FAA's UTM Concept of Operations \cite{faa2022utmconops} and the European Union's U-space framework \cite{easa2025anra} both define protocols outlined in the paper by Kopardekar, in which ASPs manage the airspace within their sector and co-ordinate with neighbouring ASPs to manage handovers of UASs between sectors. This literature provides the context for this project and the basis for the design of the simulation framework. \par

\vspace{1em}

A growing body of reseach appling MAPF techniques to UTM has emerged in recent years. one line of research by Ho et al. \cite{ho2019mapf, ho202decentralised, ho2022mapf} progressivly develops a range of algorithms for co-ordinating UAS traffic focusing on decentralised execution joint with centralised planning. Another line of research by Sarcharny and Henderson \cite{sarcharny2022strategic} focuses on a line-based architecture in which airspaces are restrcted to predefined corridors. not only does this make the problem more tractable but it also  allows for segment based reservations which are more efficient then uavs reserving individual grid cells as it reduces deconfliction from a high dimentioal space time seach to a one dimentional scheduling problem. This system is similar to the one adopted in this project, however the focus of this project is on the comparative performance of different policies for co-ordinating UAS traffic, rather than the development of a single novel algorithm. \par

\vspace{1em}

despite active reseach within the UTM literature, the comparative performance of different policies with there Satey and efficiency trade-offs under realistic demand patterns is not yet well understood. Exisiting work tents to focus on either an optimal centralised solution and specific architeures rather then an emirical evalutation of candidate policies.  \par



\subsection{Reservation-based deconfliction and reactive deconfliction}
\label{sec:reservation}

Conflict avoidance in multi-agent systems can be approached at two layers. At plan time, when agensts commit in adance plans that are contructed to be conflict free, and at execution time, when agents adjust there motion reactivly in response to observed conflict risk. Operational UTM frameowkrs combine both with strategic deconfliction policies in planning and tactical deconfliction policies that can handle the uncertainty and dynamic nature that is inherant to real world UAS operations. \cite{faa2022utmconops}. This subscetion exemaines both layers and how they will relate to the design adopted in the project. \par

\vspace{1em}

The reservation table introduced by Silver \cite{silver2005cooperative} is the fundimental mechanism for strategic deconfliction in MAPF. it indexes vertex time pairs that represent the current and planned occupation of a vertex at a given times. Agents can query the reservation table to check for potential conflicts with other agents, and can update the table when they commit to a plan. This mechanism is used in many MAPF algorithms, including the ones evaluated in this project, as it provides a simple and efficient way to manage conflicts at plan time. \par

\vspace{1em}

However, as the planning horizon increases (how far into the future agents plan), the reservation table and thus the complexity of finding conflict free paths increases substntially. Phillips and likhachev \cite{phillips2011sipp} addresses this with safe interval planning by using "safe time intervals" to represent the time periods in which a vertex is free of conflicts, rather then using a reservation table that tracks the occupation of each vertex at each time step. \par

\vspace{1em}

The reseravtion table is also limted as it relies on agents commiting to plans in advanced, assuming they will not change and as the planning horizon increases the complexity of finding and checking for a conflict free path increases substantially, and the risk of a future event chaning the environment and invalidating the plan increases. This highlights the need for reactive deconfliction which will be discussed later in this section. \par

\vspace{1em}

The reservation mechanism implemented in this project is a segment based reservation system, in which agents reserve segments of the airspace rather than individual grid cells. This is more efficient as it reduces the dimensionality of the search space for conflict free paths.\par
