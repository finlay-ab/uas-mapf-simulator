\section{Introduction}

The comercial use of Unmanned Aerial Systems (UASs) in urban environments is expanding rapidly, with applications ranging from goods delivery and infrastucture inspection to survalance and sports coverage. \par

\vspace{1em}

Industry projections and regulatory road maps anticipate that the density of low altitude urban air traffic will within the next decade exceed what can be co-ordantied by human Air Traffic Contorllers (ATCs). This is why governments and regulatory bodies have developed frameworks for automated UAS Traffic Managment (UTM): in May 2025, the European Union Aviation Safety Agency certified its first U-space Service Provider \cite{easa2025anra}, and the United kingdom Civial Aviation Authoriy has published targets aimed at enabling urban UTM by 2028 \cite{caa2024bvlos}. Despite this the technical challenges of developing such an automated sytem and how it should perform remains an area of active research. \par

\vspace{1em}

A central part of the UTM problem is Airspace Service providers (ASPs) must be able to co-ordinate the flight plan requests within a defined sector of urban airspace \cite{kopardekar2014utm, faa2022utmconops}. Multiple ASPs are expected to operate concurrently in a networked system, managing the handover of of UASs between sectors by following well-defined protocols. The policies and protocols by which ASPs give autherisation decisions fundimentially shape the saftey and efficiency of the UTM system, yet the comparative behaviour of different policies under realistic demand patterns is not yet well understood. \par

\vspace{1em}

This project frames the problem of co-ordinating UASs through ASPs as a Multi-Agent Path Finding (MAPF) problem.The problem of finding collision free paths for multiple agents sharing a descrete environment \cite{stern2019multi}. MAPF is a well studied problem with a large body of literature on algorithms for saftey, optimality and scalablity of different automated management policies. Aswell as a substantial number of potential candiadate algorithms ranging from simple heuristic searches to complex conflict resolving procedures. Treating the UTM problem as a MAPF problem makes it possable to evaluate polices systematially under quantified demand patterns to compare and contrast there performance (rather then judging them on intuition). \par

\vspace{1em}

To support this evaluation, a custom discrete-event simulaiton framework has been developed in python using SimPy. The simulaiton models a two-dimensional urban airspace divided into multiple grid sectors, each managed by an independed ASP and connected to its neighours by directional gates that abstract the handover protocols described in the UTM literature. UAVs operate as lifelong agents (as introduced by Ma et al \cite{ma2020lifelong}), picking up jobs from designated depots and travelling between depots and destinations that may be located within there orgin sector or in a different sector. Routing is structured heirarchically; a high level planner that resolves the airspaces the UAV must pass through, and a low level planner which is responsable for finding a path within a sector. The low level planner is suplimented with optional collision avoidance polices such as reservation based deconfliction and Recipricol Velocity Obstacles (RVO) \par

\vspace{1em}

The aim of this project is to evalutae the comparative performance of automated airpsace manegment polices for ASP controlled urban UAS traffic, with particular focus to the saftey and efficiency trade-off that distinguishes one policy from another. The associated objectives are to design and imliment a multi-ASP discrete-event simultion frameowrk, to define quantitative perfromance metrics coverving both saftey (seperation violations) and efficientcy (timeliness and throughput), to impliment a range of management policies, and to conduct a systematic evaluation of these polices under varying demand patterns and constraints. \par

\vspace{1em}

\subsection{Single-agent path planning}
\label{sec:single-agent-planning}

Single-agent path planning, the problem of computing a route between two points in a given environment subject to constraints (such as obstacles and cost functions), has been extensively studied as early as the 1950s and provides the foundation for the multi-agent path finding problem. This subsection introduces teh algorithms that appear as componets of the multi-agent path finding polcies evaluated in this project, and the literature on which they are based. \par

\vspace{1em}

Breadth-First Search (BFS) and Depth-First Search (DFS) are the most basic uninformed search algorithms for path planning \cite{cormen2022clrs}. BFS returns the path with the fewst steps from start to goal on a uniform cost grid, while DFS explores each branch to the maximum depth before backtracking. while both algorithms are complete neither is optimal, and both can be inefficient in large search spaces. they have been included in this project as a baseline for comparison with more sophiticated algorithms and to provide a measureable benchmark for which more complex algorithms can be compared against. \par

\vspace{1em}

The A* algorithm dominates the literature on single-agent path planning and is widely used in practice \cite{hart1968astar}. A* augments Dijkstras algorithm with a heuristic estimate of the remaining cost from each point to the goal, and is guaranteed to return an optimal path whenever this heuristic is admissible (never overestimates the true cost). By focusing the seach toward the goal rather than expanding all possible paths, therefor making A* substantially more efficient than uninformed algorithms like BFS and DFS. while retianing the optimality guaranteees. The implimentation in this project uses the euclidean distance (the straight-line distance) to the goal as its heuristic. \par

\vspace{1em}

While the A* algorithm is optimal for pathfinding, it doesnt account for the dynamic nature of the environment, such as moving obstacles or changing conditions. This is where the D* algorithm comes in \cite{stentz1994dstar} D* is an extension of A* that allows for replanning in dynamic environments. It maintains a cost map of the environment and updates it as new information becomes available, allowing it to efficiently replan paths when obstacles are detected or conditions change. Within the context of this project, D* lite is used which is an augmentation of D* that is more efficient and easier to implement  \cite{koenig2004dstar}. \par
